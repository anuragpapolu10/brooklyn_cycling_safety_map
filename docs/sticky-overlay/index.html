Brooklyn's Cycling Danger Zones

And what the city is doing about it.

Brooklyn has just been crowned the best large city for cycling in the US. We've done it. My cyclist friends and I will sit around in silence while sipping beers as there's nothing for us to talk about anymore. I too biked, Brooklyn of ample hills was mine.

Behold, the best cycling network in any large city in the US.

Look at those beautiful green lines, interlocking so densely to protect cyclists from any harm that might come to them!

But beneath the surface of this new network lies a ghost map- a history of crashes that tells a different story.

This is a heatmap of cyclists injuries and deaths from July 2012 to June 2025 according to NYPD data. That's 18,455 injuries, and 71 deaths, averaging at 4 injuries per day.

Here are some of the zones with the highest volume of crashes

It's not that these zones don't have bikelanes. Only the Broadway zone doesn't have a bike lane. But it shows that bike lanes alone don't solve the problem.

The Grand Street bike lane is currently the most heavily protected of these. To understand why crashes continue to occur despite trying, let's take a closer look.

This is what the old unprotected bike lane looked like. To drivers, the green color of the bike lane was too tempting not to park on.

To stop vehicles from parking in the bike lane, in 2020, the NYC Department of Transportation installed some bendy sticks to increase protection after years of delays. They didn't help.

Concrete barriers were installed in 2022. vehicles continued to park at the beginning of barriers anyway.

Can't even get into or out of the bike lane now! It's kind of funny, but then we have to remember it's a matter of life and death!

This illustrates the problem of what the DoT calls adjacent crashes.

89% of crashes happen at intersections.

This includes adjacent crashes, as well as right angle crashes.

And also vehicle turn crashes. (My least favourite, personnaly.)

In a city of grids, there is no choice but to find ways to make intersections safer.

Starting in 2016, the NYC DoT started to build "traffic calming measures" to slow down road users and increase visibility while turning.

Although I don't have cyclist specific data. I can say from experience that they-

1. stop cars from cutting the corner while turning.

2. Provide a safe place to stop for any reason.

Here are all the locations where they have been installed in Brooklyn.

A recent DoT study on universal daylighting showed that a variety of measures need to be taken to reduce crashes.

Ultimately, the things that makes pedestrians safe are also the things that can make cyclists safer.

Despite being the best large city for cycling in the US. Brooklyn's still got a long way to go when it comes to cycling safety.

So until then, riders still have to ride safely and be aware of their surroundings.

Come ride some of them with me so you don't have to! You can take a safer side street.

This is Bedford Ave and Martense st in Flatbush.

I've had a really close call here before, and you can see why, it's a major road, no protection, and cyclists probably have to ride around the bus when it's at the bus stop.

This is Broadway and Thornton at the Broadway Triangle.

No bike lane here, an infamous (for blocking bike lanes) Piece of Cake Moving truck is parked here, the intersection is at an awkward angle, making it easier to misjudge the situation. The train track pillar is also blocking the view to look right.

This is 4th Ave and 41st Street in Sunset Park

4th Ave is wide, and well known for cars making turns at high speeds. This is dangerous because the bike lane is protected by parked cars (visible on the left edge of the video). So the cyclist and the turning driver couldn't see each other. The recently installed empty space helps a lot to improve visibility, but that space is often used as parking too.

This is Roebling St and South 4th St in Williamsburg.

While Roebling doesn't have a bike lane, South 4th has a really nice one. I think the issue here is cyclists don't really want to stop here on red lights. There's also some very hard to spot rubber speed bumps on THornton. But this is a weird one.

So this is the worst of the worst of Brooklyn's Bike infrastructure.

America's best large city for cycling!
